Programs vs. Systems: Why Detroit Keeps Starting Over

Detroit doesn't need more programs. It needs systems that actually work. There's a difference—and understanding it changes everything.

Jonathan Barlow Life Engineer

I've watched Detroit launch another workforce development program. Another small business initiative. Another neighborhood revitalization effort. The press releases sound the same as they did five years ago, ten years ago.

Meanwhile, the same problems persist because we keep building programs instead of systems.

Programs Are Projects, Systems Are Infrastructure

A program has a start date, an end date, and a budget. It exists to solve a specific problem for a specific group of people. When the funding runs out or the political winds shift, the program disappears.

A system is infrastructure. It's built to handle volume, designed for longevity, and creates predictable outcomes. Systems don't depend on heroic effort or charismatic leadership to function.

Detroit has run countless job training programs. Most participants can't tell you where to apply those skills after graduation because there's no systematic connection between training and actual job placement. The program checked its boxes. The system was never built.

Programs create activity. Systems create outcomes.

The Real Cost of Program Thinking

Every program Detroit launches requires new staff, new marketing, new enrollment, new tracking systems. We spend enormous energy getting programs off the ground, only to watch them wind down when the grant period ends.

Then we start over. New program, new name, same fundamental gap.

The cost isn't just financial—though that's significant. The real cost is institutional knowledge that never accumulates, relationships that don't compound, and trust that erodes with every false start.

When a small business owner has participated in three different "small business support programs" over five years, and each one disappeared, they stop believing in the next one. Can you blame them?

Building Systems That Actually Work

A system approach asks different questions:

  • What infrastructure can we build once that serves thousands of people indefinitely?
  • How do we design for longevity, not just launch?
  • What happens when the current leadership changes?
  • How do we make success automatic rather than heroic?

The best economic development I've seen doesn't come from programs. It comes from systems: clear pathways, automated processes, institutional relationships that persist regardless of who's in office.

Consider the difference between a "small business mentorship program" and a systematic connection between every new business license applicant and a network of established businesses. One requires ongoing program management. The other runs on autopilot once built.

Systems Create Compound Returns

Programs create linear impact: spend X, help Y people, end.

Systems create compound returns. Every business that succeeds through a systematic support network becomes part of that network. Every person hired through systematic pathways strengthens those pathways. The system gets better with use.

Detroit doesn't lack for caring people or good intentions. We have both in abundance. What we lack is infrastructure—the boring, unsexy, persistent systems that turn good intentions into reliable outcomes.

Until we stop chasing the next program and start building systems that last, we'll keep having the same conversations, launching the same initiatives, and wondering why we're still solving the same problems.

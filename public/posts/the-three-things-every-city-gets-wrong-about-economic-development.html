The Three Things Every City Gets Wrong About Economic Development

Jonathan Barlow Life Engineer
April 25, 2026 5 min read

Austin spent $18.7 billion on corporate incentives between 2005-2015, yet small businesses still account for 99.9% of all enterprises and employ 47.1% of the workforce nationwide. We keep betting on the wrong horse. After working with economic development agencies across multiple cities, the same three systemic errors appear everywhere—from Detroit's comeback efforts to Phoenix's sprawl management to Austin's tech boom chase.

Error One: Chasing Headlines Instead of Fundamentals

Cities love the big announcement. The Fortune 500 relocation. The tech campus. The automotive plant. These make mayors look effective and generate positive press coverage. But they represent economic development theater, not economic development strategy.

Detroit provides the clearest example. While the city rightfully celebrated major investments like the new Hudson's site and Ford's Corktown campus, the real economic momentum came from something less photogenic: fixing basic infrastructure and streamlining permitting processes. Small manufacturers and service businesses didn't need tax breaks—they needed reliable electricity, predictable permit timelines, and functioning roads.

Phoenix made the opposite mistake during its growth years, approving every major development proposal while neglecting the water infrastructure and transportation networks that would actually sustain long-term economic activity. The result: a metropolitan area that struggles with basic service delivery despite billions in corporate development.

The fundamentals aren't sexy. Zoning reform. Utility reliability. Permit processing speed. But these determine whether businesses can actually operate once they arrive. We've seen cities spend millions recruiting companies that leave within five years because the operational basics never worked.

Error Two: Treating Economic Development as a Department Instead of a System

Most cities organize economic development as a single department with a mandate to attract businesses. This creates immediate structural problems. Economic development isn't something one department does—it's an outcome of how well all city systems function together.

A business decides to locate somewhere based on talent availability, infrastructure quality, regulatory predictability, and market access. The economic development department controls none of these directly. Yet we expect them to deliver results while the planning department restricts density, the utilities department runs unreliable service, and the permitting department creates months-long approval processes.

Detroit's revival accelerated when leadership started treating economic development as a systems coordination problem. Instead of just marketing the city, they aligned housing policy, transportation planning, workforce development, and business services. When a company expresses interest, they can now provide concrete answers about talent pipelines, infrastructure capacity, and regulatory timelines.

This systems approach explains why some smaller cities outperform larger ones in economic development. They can coordinate across departments more easily, creating coherent value propositions instead of mixed messages.

Economic development isn't something one department does—it's an outcome of how well all city systems function together.

Error Three: Optimizing for Metrics That Don't Matter

Economic development agencies track jobs created, capital investment attracted, and incentive dollars deployed. These numbers make good press releases but reveal little about actual economic health. They're lagging indicators of decisions made years earlier, not real-time measures of competitive position.

The metrics that actually predict economic growth are harder to measure but more important: business formation rates, permit processing times, infrastructure reliability scores, and talent retention percentages. A city might celebrate landing a 500-job manufacturer while ignoring that fifty small businesses left due to regulatory friction.

Austin's experience illustrates this metric mismatch perfectly. The city successfully attracted major tech companies and celebrated the job creation numbers. But housing costs rose so fast that the service workers and middle-income professionals who support those tech companies couldn't afford to live there. The celebrated success created its own systemic failure.

We need economic development metrics that measure system health, not just system outputs. How long does it take to start a business? What percentage of graduates from local universities stay in the region? How reliable are utilities during peak demand? These metrics indicate whether a local economy can sustain growth or just experience periodic boom-bust cycles.

What Actually Works

Cities that consistently grow their economic base focus on three areas: reducing friction for existing businesses, maintaining infrastructure that scales, and developing talent that matches their economic strategy. None of these generate immediate headlines, but all create compounding advantages over time.

Reducing friction means streamlining regulations without eliminating necessary oversight, maintaining predictable tax structures, and providing reliable city services. Detroit's recovery gained momentum when the city moved from defending against decline to systematically removing barriers to business operation.

Infrastructure that scales means building transportation networks, utility systems, and housing stock that can support growth without creating bottlenecks. This requires long-term capital planning aligned with economic development goals—something most cities separate into different departments with different funding cycles.

Talent development means aligning educational institutions, workforce training programs, and immigration policies with the types of businesses the city wants to attract and retain. This isn't about recruiting workers from other cities—it's about developing local human capital that creates sustained competitive advantages.

The cities that will thrive over the next decade won't be the ones that win the most corporate relocations or deploy the most creative incentive packages. They'll be the ones that make it easy for businesses to start, operate, and grow while developing the infrastructure and talent needed to support sustained economic activity. That's systems thinking applied to economic development—and it's how cities actually build lasting prosperity instead of just managing press cycles.

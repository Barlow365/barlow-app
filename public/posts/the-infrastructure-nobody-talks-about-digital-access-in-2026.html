The Infrastructure Nobody Talks About: Digital Access in 2026

Jonathan Barlow Life Engineer
April 14, 2026 5 min read

Twenty-one percent of American households still lack reliable broadband internet access in 2024, according to the Federal Communications Commission. That's not a telecommunications problem—it's an infrastructure failure that undermines every other system we're trying to build.

The Hidden Foundation Layer

We measure infrastructure success by the visibility of concrete and steel. Roads, bridges, water lines—the physical systems that move people and resources. But digital infrastructure operates as the nervous system beneath everything else we're constructing.

Detroit illustrates this perfectly. The city invested heavily in downtown revitalization, creating impressive physical spaces and attracting businesses. Yet large swaths of residential neighborhoods remain digitally isolated, creating a two-tier economy within the same metropolitan area. You can't participate in modern commerce, education, or civic engagement without consistent internet access.

This isn't about convenience anymore. Digital access functions as critical infrastructure, determining which communities can access remote work opportunities, online education, telemedicine, and basic government services. When we build without considering connectivity, we're constructing incomplete systems.

The Real Costs of Digital Redlining

Internet service providers have replicated the same geographic discrimination patterns that defined 20th-century housing policy. Low-income neighborhoods in cities like Cleveland and Birmingham face significantly higher costs per megabit and lower service reliability than affluent areas just miles away.

The National Digital Inclusion Alliance found that 43% of Detroit households lack broadband subscriptions, compared to 13% in nearby suburban communities. This creates compound disadvantages: students fall behind in digital literacy, small businesses can't compete online, and residents miss economic opportunities that require internet access.

These gaps don't close naturally through market forces. Private internet providers focus investment where return on investment is highest, leaving entire neighborhoods systematically underserved. Without intervention, digital divides widen as online services become increasingly essential to daily life.

System-Level Diagnosis

The problem stems from treating internet service as a luxury rather than infrastructure. We regulate water, electricity, and gas as public utilities because they're essential for modern life. Internet access now meets the same criteria, but policy hasn't caught up to reality.

Municipal broadband initiatives in cities like Chattanooga demonstrate alternative approaches. When local governments treat connectivity as infrastructure rather than leaving it entirely to private markets, service quality improves and costs decrease. Chattanooga's publicly-owned network delivers gigabit speeds at lower prices than most private providers.

The challenge is scale and consistency. Successful municipal programs require significant upfront investment and technical expertise. Many cities lack the resources or political will to build comprehensive digital infrastructure, creating a patchwork of connected and disconnected communities.

Building the Fix

Real solutions require treating digital access as we treat other essential infrastructure: through coordinated public investment with consistent standards. This doesn't necessarily mean government-owned networks everywhere, but it does mean government involvement in ensuring universal access.

Detroit's recent partnership with local internet service providers to expand fiber coverage in underserved neighborhoods shows one path forward. The city provides streamlined permitting and infrastructure access while private companies handle installation and service. This hybrid approach leverages public assets without requiring cities to become internet service providers.

The federal Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act allocated $65 billion for broadband expansion, but most funding flows through state programs with varying effectiveness. States that treat this as infrastructure investment—with clear metrics, accountability, and long-term planning—will see better outcomes than those viewing it as economic development spending.

The Operator's Perspective

For developers and business operators, digital infrastructure affects every project decision. Site selection must consider not just physical transportation access but connectivity quality and redundancy. A warehouse in an area with limited internet options faces operational constraints regardless of other advantages.

We're seeing this play out in real estate values. Properties in areas with high-speed fiber access command premiums, while those in digitally underserved areas face discounts that compound over time. This creates feedback loops that either accelerate neighborhood improvement or decline.

Smart operators factor connectivity into their location analysis from day one. This means engaging with local internet service providers early, understanding infrastructure plans, and sometimes investing in connectivity improvements as part of development costs. Digital access has become as fundamental to site evaluation as water pressure or electrical capacity.

The cities that solve digital access systematically—treating connectivity as infrastructure rather than amenity—will have fundamental advantages in attracting residents, businesses, and investment. The question isn't whether digital infrastructure matters, but whether we'll build it intentionally or let market failures determine which communities get connected. The systems we construct today will shape economic opportunity for decades.

# Article Developer Prompts

Use these prompts to generate each article. Feed the citation library alongside.

---

## TONE RULES (ALWAYS INCLUDE)

```
Voice: Builder diagnosing systems. Not activist, not politician, not emotional.
Pattern: Facts → insight → critique → redirect → leave room for next article.
Length: 800-1200 words.
References: 2-4 stats from citation library.
POV: First person plural ("we") or observational. Light personal authority.
Cities: Reference 2-3 cities (always include Detroit + 1-2 others).
Ecosystem: Subtly reinforce multi-system operator perspective.
```

---

## ARTICLE 1: America Isn't Broke — It's Disconnected
**Format A (Data-Driven Op-Ed)**

```
Write a 1000-word op-ed titled "America Isn't Broke — It's Disconnected"

Opening: Lead with the contradiction — GDP is up, markets are up, employment
is technically strong. But 60%+ of Americans live paycheck to paycheck.
Use 2-3 stats on income vs cost of living gap.

Middle: The problem isn't money supply — it's that systems designed to
distribute opportunity (workforce, housing, small business support) operate
in silos. Money flows in, but doesn't connect to outcomes. Reference Detroit,
Atlanta, and Cleveland as examples of cities where dollars exist but
coordination doesn't.

End: Pose the question — what if the issue isn't funding, but architecture?
Leave open for next article on cost of living.
```

## ARTICLE 2: Why Cost of Living Relief Isn't Reaching Families
**Format A (Data-Driven Op-Ed)**

```
Write a 1000-word op-ed titled "Why Cost of Living Relief Isn't Reaching Families"

Opening: Inflation is cooling (cite CPI), but families aren't feeling it.
Grocery costs up 25% since 2020. Savings rate near historic lows.
Credit card debt at $1.14 trillion.

Middle: Relief programs exist — tax credits, SNAP, utility assistance — but
they're fragmented. A family in Detroit might qualify for 6 programs across
4 agencies with 4 different applications. The system creates friction, not flow.
Same pattern in Memphis, Baltimore, Philly.

End: The gap isn't generosity — it's delivery infrastructure. Programs reach
people when they're designed around how people actually live.
```

## ARTICLE 3: We Fund Startups. We Don't Build Businesses
**Format C (Field Insight)**

```
Write a 900-word field insight titled "We Fund Startups. We Don't Build Businesses"

Opening: "Working across cities and ecosystems, I've watched the same pattern
repeat..." Record business formation (5.5M applications). Accelerators, pitch
competitions, grants everywhere. But 50% close by year 5.

Middle: What's missing isn't capital — it's operational infrastructure.
Accounting, legal, HR, supply chain, customer systems. We celebrate the
launch, but nobody builds the boring middle. Reference Motor City Match,
similar programs in Atlanta and Philly.

End: The cities that will win aren't the ones with the most startups.
They're the ones that build the systems to keep businesses alive past year 3.
```

## ARTICLE 4: The Workforce System Isn't Failing — It's Misaligned
**Format B (System Breakdown)**

```
Write a 1000-word system breakdown titled "The Workforce System Isn't Failing — It's Misaligned"

Opening: Identify the visible problem — $3.6B spent on WIOA annually,
employers still say "we can't find talent," 87% report difficulty filling roles.

Middle: Show how the system actually works — training providers design
programs based on what they can deliver, not what employers need. Employers
post requirements based on ideal candidates, not trainable ones. Job seekers
navigate between disconnected providers. Show where it breaks at each handoff.

Shifts: (1) Training should start at the employer's door, not the classroom.
(2) Placement without retention is waste. (3) Data should flow between
providers, employers, and support services.

Reference Detroit, Cleveland, Baltimore workforce systems.
```

## ARTICLE 5: Training Programs Don't Create Careers — Systems Do
**Format B (System Breakdown)**

```
Write a 1000-word system breakdown titled "Training Programs Don't Create Careers — Systems Do"

Opening: Only 37% of workforce trainees are employed in their trained field
at 1 year. That's not a failure of training — it's a failure of the system
around it.

Middle: A training program is one node. A career requires: skill development →
credential → placement → onboarding → retention → advancement. Most programs
handle step 1 and maybe step 2. The other 4 steps have no owner.

Show the system map. Show where it breaks. Show what "career infrastructure"
looks like vs "training programs."

Reference cities where this gap is most visible. End with: the question
isn't "did they complete the program?" — it's "are they still employed
18 months later?"
```

## ARTICLE 6: AI Is Exposing a Bigger Problem Than Job Loss
**Format D (Forward-Looking)**

```
Write a 900-word forward-looking piece titled "AI Is Exposing a Bigger
Problem Than Job Loss"

Opening: 300 million jobs globally could be affected. 72% of companies
already using AI. The conversation is about displacement. But that's
the wrong conversation.

Middle: AI isn't just replacing tasks — it's revealing which systems were
already fragile. Customer service, data entry, content production — these
weren't strong systems. They were labor-intensive workarounds. AI accelerates
the exposure of systems that never worked well.

Forward look: Cities that already struggle with digital access (24M Americans
without broadband) will fall further behind. The AI conversation needs to
shift from "what jobs will be lost" to "what systems need to be rebuilt."

End: The real disruption isn't automation — it's that we can no longer
hide behind manual processes.
```

## SOCIAL MEDIA PROMPTS (Per Article)

### LinkedIn Post (Day 0 — Publish Day)
```
Write a 150-word LinkedIn post for [ARTICLE TITLE].
Lead with the most striking stat from the article.
Include 1 sentence of personal insight.
End with "Full article: [link]"
No hashtags. No emojis. Professional but direct.
```

### X/Twitter Post (Day 0)
```
Write a 280-character tweet for [ARTICLE TITLE].
Lead with contradiction or surprising stat.
End with link.
```

### LinkedIn Follow-Up (Day 3)
```
Write a 100-word LinkedIn post that adds NEW insight
related to [ARTICLE TITLE]. Don't repeat the article.
Frame as "Here's what I didn't say in the article..."
or "The response to this piece revealed something..."
No link needed — this is engagement bait.
```

### Instagram Caption (Day 1)
```
Write a 200-word Instagram caption for a quote graphic
from [ARTICLE TITLE]. The graphic shows the article's
strongest one-liner. Caption expands on the idea.
End with "Link in bio" and 3-5 relevant hashtags.
```

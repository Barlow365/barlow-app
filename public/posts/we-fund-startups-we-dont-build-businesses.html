We Fund Startups. We Don't Build Businesses

Jonathan Barlow Life Engineer
April 9, 2026 4 min read

Working across cities and ecosystems, I've watched the same pattern repeat in Detroit, Atlanta, and Philadelphia. Record business formation hit 5.5 million applications in 2023, according to the Census Bureau. Accelerators multiply. Pitch competitions proliferate. Grant programs expand. Yet the Small Business Administration reports that 50% of new businesses close within five years. We've built a machine that launches companies but doesn't sustain them.

The Capital Mirage

Walk through any entrepreneurship hub and you'll find the same inventory: co-working spaces, mentorship programs, and demo days. Detroit's Motor City Match has distributed $13.6 million across 350 businesses since 2014. Atlanta's Startup Runway connects minority entrepreneurs with investors. Philadelphia's StartupPHL runs accelerators and pitch competitions.

These programs solve the wrong problem. Capital scarcity ended years ago. Angel networks, micro-VCs, and crowdfunding platforms have democratized access to startup funding. The real constraint isn't getting money to start — it's building the operational backbone to survive.

The Infrastructure Gap

Most entrepreneurs launch with passion and a product. Few launch with proper accounting systems, legal compliance frameworks, or customer management infrastructure. They bootstrap these systems later, often poorly, because nobody taught them how critical the boring middle actually is.

Detroit's small manufacturers know this reality. A machining shop might have breakthrough technology, but without proper inventory management, quality control systems, and customer service infrastructure, orders become chaos. Atlanta's food entrepreneurs face the same challenge — great recipes don't automatically translate to food safety compliance, supply chain management, and distribution logistics.

We celebrate the launch but abandon businesses during the operational grind. The result: companies with market traction that collapse under their own growth because basic business systems never existed.

What Actually Kills Businesses

Cash flow problems, not capital problems, kill most businesses. According to QuickBooks' Small Business Insights, 61% of small businesses struggle with cash flow, and 32% can't pay bills on time. These aren't problems solved by another round of funding — they're problems solved by operational discipline.

Philadelphia's small business owners understand this viscerally. A corner store might generate decent revenue, but without inventory tracking, vendor payment systems, and basic financial controls, profit margins disappear into operational inefficiency. The business looks busy but bleeds money.

Legal compliance failures shut down promising companies faster than market competition. Employment law violations, tax filing mistakes, and regulatory non-compliance create existential threats that proper systems would prevent. Yet most business support programs focus on growth strategies, not compliance infrastructure.

The Systems Solution

Smart cities are quietly shifting focus from startup quantity to business durability. They're building shared service platforms that provide operational infrastructure as a utility. Accounting software subscriptions, legal compliance checklists, HR management systems, and supply chain connections become accessible resources rather than individual business expenses.

Detroit's small manufacturers increasingly share back-office services through industry cooperatives. Atlanta's food businesses access shared commercial kitchen space and regulatory guidance through coordinated programs. These aren't entrepreneurship programs — they're business infrastructure programs.

The most effective interventions happen during years two through four, not at launch. This is when businesses transition from founder-dependent operations to scalable systems. This is when operational discipline determines survival more than market opportunity.

Building for Durability

We need fewer pitch competitions and more business systems workshops. Fewer accelerators focused on fundraising and more programs teaching inventory management, customer service systems, and financial controls. The sexy part of entrepreneurship is the launch. The valuable part is the operational discipline that follows.

Cities that build this infrastructure will retain more businesses past the critical five-year mark. They'll develop ecosystems where business survival rates improve, not just business formation numbers. Success metrics should measure five-year survival rates, not first-year launches.

The cities that win long-term won't be the ones generating the most startups. They'll be the ones that understand business formation is just the beginning. Real economic development happens when we build the infrastructure that keeps businesses alive, profitable, and growing past year three. That's a different kind of ecosystem — one built for durability rather than velocity.

Why Government Contracts Favor Companies That Don't Deliver

Jonathan Barlow Life Engineer
April 23, 2026 5 min read

The federal government awarded $665 billion in contracts last year, yet studies show that 70% of major IT projects fail to meet their original specifications. We're not dealing with bad luck or incompetence—we're looking at a procurement system that systematically rewards the wrong behaviors. Companies that excel at writing proposals often struggle to execute them, while builders who could actually deliver the work get filtered out before they reach the decision table.

The Proposal Industrial Complex

Government contracts don't go to the best builders. They go to the best proposal writers. The average federal RFP response costs between $50,000 and $500,000 to prepare, creating an immediate barrier that favors large firms with dedicated proposal teams over smaller companies focused on execution.

We see this pattern across cities. Detroit's recent $50 million IT modernization contract went to a consortium that had never worked together before but assembled an impressive 200-page response. Meanwhile, local firms that had successfully delivered smaller municipal projects couldn't afford the months of unpaid work required to compete. The winning consortium is now 18 months behind schedule.

The system rewards companies that can navigate bureaucratic requirements, not ones that can solve technical problems. Proposal teams become specialists in translating generic capabilities into specific-sounding solutions. They master the art of promising everything while committing to nothing measurable.

Risk Transfer Creates Perverse Incentives

Government procurement officers face asymmetric career risk. If they choose a small, capable firm that fails, they get blamed for not following established vendor qualification procedures. If they choose a large, established firm that fails, they followed process. This creates institutional bias toward companies with track records of winning contracts, not completing them.

The result is a feedback loop that concentrates contracts among a small group of incumbent players. In Chicago, the same three consulting firms have won 60% of major technology contracts over the past decade, despite mixed delivery results. These firms maintain proposal-writing capabilities that smaller competitors simply can't match.

Large contractors have learned to game this system by subcontracting actual work to smaller firms while maintaining prime contractor status. They win contracts on paper credentials, then hire the companies that should have won directly. The client pays markup fees to middlemen who add no technical value.

The procurement process selects for companies willing to promise what they can't deliver.

The Specification Problem

Government contracts require detailed specifications written before anyone fully understands the problem. Procurement rules demand fixed-price bids on undefined scope, creating an impossible situation for honest bidders. Companies that bid realistically get undercut by those willing to promise unrealistic timelines and budgets.

In Baltimore's recent transit app development project, the winning bid was 40% lower than the next closest competitor and promised delivery in half the time. Eighteen months later, the project budget has tripled and the timeline has doubled. The low bidder knew their original proposal was impossible but counted on change orders to reach profitability.

This dynamic punishes companies that try to accurately estimate work. Honest bidders who account for likely complications and realistic timelines consistently lose to those who submit optimistic proposals and plan to renegotiate later. The procurement process selects for companies willing to promise what they can't deliver.

Measurement Failure

Government contracts focus on process compliance rather than outcome delivery. Success gets measured by adherence to predefined milestones rather than whether the final system actually works. This creates incentives for contractors to deliver technically compliant solutions that miss the point entirely.

We track contract awards and milestone completions but rarely measure whether delivered systems solve their intended problems. A Government Accountability Office study found that 32% of completed IT projects were never actually deployed for their intended use, despite being considered 'successful' from a contract management perspective.

The absence of meaningful performance measurement means there's no institutional learning. Agencies continue awarding contracts to vendors with poor delivery track records because the system doesn't connect past performance with future contract decisions in any meaningful way.

What Works: Alternative Models

Some jurisdictions are experimenting with different approaches. Outcome-based contracts tie payment to results rather than deliverables. Rapid prototyping processes test vendor capabilities on small projects before awarding larger ones. These methods favor builders over proposal writers.

The most successful government technology projects we've studied used iterative development with regular go/no-go decision points. Instead of betting everything on upfront specifications, these projects started small and scaled what worked. This approach naturally selects for vendors who can actually build things.

Detroit's recent digital services team has moved toward this model, running competitive prototyping sessions where vendors demonstrate capabilities rather than just describing them. Early results suggest this approach identifies better partners while reducing overall project risk.

Fixing government procurement requires changing how we measure success and assign risk. Until we reward actual delivery over proposal quality, we'll continue seeing public resources flow to companies optimized for winning contracts rather than completing them. The cost isn't just wasted money—it's the opportunity cost of solutions that never get built because the builders never get the chance to try.

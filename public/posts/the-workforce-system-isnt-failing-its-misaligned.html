The Workforce System Isn't Failing — It's Misaligned

Jonathan Barlow Life Engineer
April 9, 2026 4 min read

We spend $3.6 billion annually through the Workforce Innovation and Opportunity Act, yet 87% of employers report difficulty filling roles. The numbers suggest system failure, but that misses the actual problem. The workforce development system works exactly as designed — it's just designed for the wrong outcomes.

Where the Handoffs Break

Training providers build programs around what they can deliver, not what employers need. A community college in Detroit offers a six-month manufacturing certificate because they have the equipment and instructors. Meanwhile, local manufacturers need workers trained on specific automation systems they use, but that training doesn't exist.

Employers post job requirements based on wish-list candidates, not trainable ones. They ask for three years of experience in roles that didn't exist three years ago. HR departments write requirements that sound comprehensive but filter out the exact people workforce programs are designed to help.

Job seekers navigate between disconnected providers with no shared information system. Someone might complete soft skills training at a nonprofit, technical training at a community college, and job placement services through a different contractor. None of these providers know what the others are doing.

The Training-First Problem

Most workforce programs start with training and hope employment follows. This sequence creates a fundamental mismatch. Providers design curricula based on available resources, faculty expertise, and regulatory requirements — not current job openings.

In Cleveland's manufacturing corridor, workforce providers offer welding programs while local plants need workers trained on CNC machinery and quality control software. The training exists, the jobs exist, but they're operating in parallel universes.

Baltimore's workforce system exemplifies this disconnect. Multiple providers offer similar customer service training programs, each serving 20-30 participants per cohort. Local call centers, meanwhile, hire in batches of 100+ and need workers trained on their specific software platforms and protocols.

The workforce system works exactly as designed — it's just designed for the wrong outcomes.

Placement Without Retention Wastes Resources

Success metrics focus on job placement rates, not retention or advancement. Providers get paid for putting people into jobs, not keeping them employed or helping them progress. This creates perverse incentives throughout the system.

A workforce program might place someone in a $12/hour warehouse job and claim success, even if that person quits within three months because they lack transportation to get there consistently. The real failure isn't the placement — it's the absence of wraparound support that makes employment sustainable.

We track training completions and initial placements obsessively while ignoring the data that actually matters: Are people still employed six months later? Are they earning enough to support themselves? Are they developing skills that lead to advancement?

Data Silos Block System Intelligence

Information doesn't flow between the providers, employers, and support services that job seekers need. A person might receive job training through one agency, childcare assistance through another, and transportation support through a third. None of these agencies share data or coordinate services.

Employers know which training programs produce workers who succeed in their environments, but this intelligence stays trapped within individual companies. Meanwhile, training providers operate without feedback loops about how their graduates perform on the job.

Detroit's workforce ecosystem includes dozens of providers, but there's no shared database of participant progress, employer feedback, or program outcomes. Each provider optimizes for their own metrics while the overall system generates waste and inefficiency.

Three Shifts That Fix the Alignment

First, training should start at the employer's door. Instead of generic programs hoping to match market demand, we build specific training for specific jobs with specific employers. This means smaller, more targeted cohorts designed around real openings.

Second, we measure success by retention and advancement, not just placement. Providers should be accountable for outcomes six and twelve months after program completion. This shifts incentives toward sustainable employment rather than quick placements.

Third, data needs to flow between all system components. Employers should share feedback about program graduates. Training providers should know which support services their participants need. Support services should understand how their interventions affect employment outcomes.

These aren't radical changes. They're system adjustments that align incentives with desired outcomes. The infrastructure exists; we just need to rewire the connections between its components. When training starts with employers, success measures retention, and data flows freely, the same $3.6 billion investment produces dramatically different results.

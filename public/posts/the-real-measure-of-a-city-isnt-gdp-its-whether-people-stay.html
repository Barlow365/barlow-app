The Real Measure of a City Isn't GDP — It's Whether People Stay

Jonathan Barlow Life Engineer
April 1, 2026 4 min read

Detroit lost 1.1 million residents between 1950 and 2020 — a 74% population decline that makes it the poster child for urban failure. But while economists obsess over GDP growth and job creation numbers, they're measuring the wrong thing. The most honest indicator of a city's health isn't how much wealth it generates or how many businesses it attracts. It's whether people choose to stay.

The Retention Test

Population retention cuts through the noise of economic development marketing. A city can subsidize corporate headquarters, build flashy downtown districts, and generate impressive revenue numbers while hemorrhaging the people who actually live there. We've seen this playbook across the Rust Belt — Pittsburgh attracts tech workers who leave after two years, Cleveland builds stadiums while neighborhoods empty out.

Detroit's story isn't unique in its decline, but it's instructive in its honesty. When people leave en masse, they're voting with their feet on whether a place works. They're aggregating thousands of individual decisions about schools, safety, opportunity, and quality of life into a single, undeniable metric.

The retention test works both ways. Cities that keep people — even without explosive growth — signal something fundamental is working. Austin didn't become Austin because of its GDP. It became Austin because people moved there and stayed, creating the conditions that eventually drove economic growth.

What Keeps People vs What Attracts Business

Corporate attraction strategies focus on tax incentives, infrastructure, and workforce availability. People stay for different reasons: their kids can walk to good schools, crime doesn't dominate daily calculations, basic services function predictably. The gap between these two sets of priorities explains why so many economic development wins feel hollow.

Take downtown Detroit's recovery. The corporate relocations and stadium investments created genuine momentum in a concentrated area. But the real test isn't whether Quicken Loans moved downtown — it's whether the employees they hired are still living in the metro area five years later. Early indicators suggest mixed results.

Phoenix offers a contrasting example. Its GDP growth looks impressive on paper, but the city struggles with resident retention as housing costs surge. People arrive for jobs and leave when the economics stop making sense. The churn undermines the social cohesion and institutional knowledge that stable cities need.

People stay in places that work at a human scale, not in places that work on spreadsheets.

The Infrastructure of Staying

People stay in places that work at a human scale. This means predictable services, functional schools, and neighborhoods where investment makes sense. Detroit's challenge isn't just fixing what's broken — it's creating conditions where residents believe their long-term bets will pay off.

The city has made tangible progress on this front. Property tax foreclosure reforms stabilized homeownership. Improved streetlight coverage changed the daily experience of living there. These aren't glamorous fixes, but they address the basic reliability people need to commit to a place.

We're running a real-time experiment in urban systems across multiple cities. The places that get the fundamentals right — regardless of their GDP growth rate — tend to keep people. The places that prioritize metrics over lived experience continue losing residents despite their economic development successes.

Measuring What Matters

Population retention isn't just about raw numbers. It's about understanding who stays and why. Young families staying signals different strengths than retirees aging in place. College graduates remaining after school indicates something different than longtime residents who can't afford to leave.

Detroit's population has stabilized around 670,000 people, down from its 1950 peak of 1.8 million. But the composition is shifting. The city is retaining more college graduates than in previous decades, while the suburban flight patterns are changing. These are early indicators of systemic improvement that GDP measurements miss.

The retention lens also reveals policy priorities. Instead of chasing the next corporate headquarters, cities might focus on the conditions that make people want to build lives somewhere: housing policy that supports homeownership, schools that families trust, infrastructure that reliably serves residents.

Cities are ultimately about people choosing to build lives together in the same place over time. Detroit's decline taught us what happens when those conditions break down. Its stabilization offers lessons about what it takes to rebuild them. The next article will examine how housing policy specifically supports or undermines that choice to stay.

The Operator Gap: Why Execution Talent Is the Scarcest Resource

Jonathan Barlow Life Engineer
April 21, 2026 5 min read

According to CB Insights, 70% of startups fail not because their idea was wrong, but because they couldn't execute it properly. We have more MBAs than ever, more consultants, more strategy frameworks. What we lack are operators—people who can take a plan and make it work in the messy reality of markets, regulations, and human behavior.

The Visibility Problem

Strategy gets the spotlight. Operations gets the basement office. This isn't just corporate bias—it reflects how we fundamentally misunderstand value creation. The CEO who articulates vision gets profiled in Forbes. The VP of Operations who builds the systems that deliver on that vision remains anonymous.

In Detroit, we see this pattern across the economic development landscape. City officials announce grand plans for downtown revitalization or neighborhood transformation. The announcement generates headlines. But the real work—coordinating permitting, managing contractor relationships, ensuring quality control—happens in the shadows, often by people whose names never appear in press releases.

This visibility gap creates a talent pipeline problem. Ambitious people gravitate toward roles with clear recognition paths. Operations roles, despite being mission-critical, often feel like dead ends. The result: we optimize for strategy creation over strategy execution.

The Skills Mismatch

Business schools teach case studies. Operations requires pattern recognition built from repeated contact with failure. The skillset gap isn't just technical—it's experiential. You can't learn how to troubleshoot supply chain disruptions from a textbook. You learn by managing through the 2021 chip shortage, the 2020 logistics breakdown, the 2008 credit freeze.

Austin's tech boom illustrates this perfectly. The city has attracted thousands of engineers and product managers from Silicon Valley, but struggles to find operations leaders who understand both technology scaling and Texas regulations. The result: promising companies that can build great products but can't navigate local permitting, labor markets, or distribution networks.

Charlotte's financial sector faces similar challenges. Banks can hire analysts and relationship managers, but finding operations leaders who understand both fintech innovation and banking compliance proves consistently difficult. The regulatory complexity requires deep operational experience, not just analytical ability.

We have more MBAs than ever, more consultants, more strategy frameworks. What we lack are operators who can take a plan and make it work in messy reality.

The Complexity Multiplier

Modern business operations exist in a web of interdependencies that didn't exist twenty years ago. Supply chains span continents. Regulatory frameworks change quarterly. Customer expectations reset monthly. The operator today manages not just internal processes, but external ecosystem relationships.

Consider what it takes to open a restaurant in Detroit versus 1990. Then: secure location, hire staff, order food, open doors. Now: navigate zoning requirements, manage multiple delivery app relationships, comply with health protocols, coordinate social media presence, optimize for review platforms, handle digital payments, manage inventory through supply chain disruptions.

Each additional layer of complexity exponentially increases the difficulty of execution. Yet we continue to approach operations hiring as if it's 1990. We look for people who can manage the restaurant, not people who can orchestrate the ecosystem.

The Training Deficit

Most companies promote their best individual contributors into operations roles. This creates a predictable pattern: high performers get overwhelmed by the shift from doing work to orchestrating work, burnout or underperform, and either leave or get replaced. The cycle repeats.

The problem isn't individual capacity. It's training infrastructure. According to the National Association of Manufacturers, 83% of manufacturers report difficulty finding qualified operations talent, but only 31% have formal development programs for operations roles. We expect people to learn complex systems management through trial and error.

Detroit's manufacturing renaissance suffers from this exact gap. Plants need operators who understand both traditional manufacturing and modern automation, both union dynamics and global supply chains. But most training programs focus on either technical skills or management theory, not the integrated systems thinking that modern operations requires.

The Market Signal

Compensation structures reveal our true priorities. Strategy consultants at McKinsey start around $165,000. Operations managers at Fortune 500 companies average $89,000. The market signals that thinking about operations matters more than actually doing operations.

This compensation gap becomes self-reinforcing. High-potential candidates choose strategy roles over operations roles. Operations becomes a landing spot for people who couldn't get strategy jobs, rather than a destination for people who love building systems. Quality declines, reinforcing the perception that operations is less valuable than strategy.

The companies that buck this trend—Amazon, Toyota, Southwest Airlines—consistently outperform their sectors. They've figured out how to attract and retain execution talent by treating operations as a core competency rather than a support function.

The operator gap isn't just a hiring problem—it's a systems design problem. Until we restructure how we identify, develop, and reward execution talent, we'll continue building economies heavy on vision and light on delivery. The cities and companies that solve this first will have a decade-long competitive advantage. The question isn't whether this talent exists, but whether we're creating the conditions for it to thrive.
